Saturn V is one of the most technically advanced human creations in history. It was a 110-meter high rocket, 10 meters in diameter and able to carry 118 tons of cargo at the LEO. It uses F-1 and J-2 engines. During tests, the engine noise was audible within a radius of 80 km.
The Soviet answer to Saturn V was the N1 rocket. Saturn V was taller, heavier and produced a larger thrust during takeoff, but the first stage N1 had a larger diameter. The N1 rocket performed four test flights before the end of the program, but all ended in failure. The first Saturn V stage had 5 F-1 engines with enormous power, while N1 was equipped with 30 small engines. During two starts, Apollo 6 and Apollo 13 there was a decrease in the power of one of the engines, but the computer managed to align the string. N1 was designed in a hurry and the engines were never tested at the same time, which caused catastrophes during takeoffs. If the first stage N1 was tested separately, these shortcomings could be avoided.
